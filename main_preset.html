About this artwork

Utagawa Kunisada, aka Toyokuni III Cherry blossoms at Genji’s Rokujō Mansion 1854

The scene here depicts a cherry blossom viewing party. The Japanese call flower viewing hanami – which was and still is a spring tradition. People celebrated by feasting with friends and family under the blooming trees. Hanami was such an important custom that the Tokugawa shogun rulers planted hundreds of cherry trees (sakura) in parks and temples so that people from all walks of life could admire them.

This picture was designed by Kunisada. He was one of the most prolific ukiyo-e artists of his time. This scene is from the book False Murasaki’s Rural Genji, a parody of the popular 11th-century novel Tale of Genji.

Fun fact: Cherry blossoms are especially precious because of their short lifespan. Around two weeks after blooming, their petals are whisked away with the wind. These delicate flowers are sometimes seen as a metaphor for life: beautiful yet fleeting. As a result, hanami is a time for reflecting on the beauty of impermanent things. The Japanese use the phrase “mono no aware” (literally, “the feeling of things”) when talking about the emotions certain things can spark in us.
